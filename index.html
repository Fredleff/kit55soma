KIT55 SOMA

The project doesn’t bleed because the contractors are incompetent.
It bleeds because the structure between them has no spine.

In multi-prime environments, every party is contractually isolated. Their incentives are aligned to protect their own scope and margin — not the outcome of the whole. This is not a people problem. It is a structural one.

THE STRUCTURE

What actually happens when no one is responsible for the space between contracts.

When procurement, manufacturing, logistics, and installation are awarded as separate primes, the system becomes loosely coupled by design. Each actor can optimize their own piece without regard for what happens downstream or upstream.

The interfaces between them are not collaborative touchpoints. They are blame boundaries.

When material arrives late, procurement points at shipping. When installation fails, the installer points at materials. When the schedule collapses, the owner absorbs the cost because no contract penalizes the system for failing — only the individual scopes for missing their isolated targets.

Contractual Isolation

Each subcontractor’s agreement is written to minimize their own risk and maximize their own margin. No contract rewards them for reducing the total cost of the project. Cooperation is economically irrational under these terms.

Loose Coupling

The parts are easy to swap, but the whole is fragile. Because there is no mechanism forcing information or accountability across boundaries, one delay or defect cascades without resistance.

Disaggregated Execution

Information stays trapped inside each firm. Trust is low. Visibility is partial. The project moves forward as a series of hand-offs rather than a coordinated sequence.

This is not theory. It is arithmetic.

These are not failures of execution. They are failures of architecture. The system is designed to produce exactly this outcome.

OPERATIONAL CONVERGENCE

We do not turn independent contractors into one team.
We force their independent actions to land on the same target.

The goal is not cultural cohesion. It is target-aligned execution across autonomous entities that have no natural incentive to cooperate.

We install the missing mechanism: a layer that makes the cost of misalignment visible and expensive in real time, while making the benefit of convergence economically rational for every party.

This requires three things most projects never have:

1. A single source of truth that cannot be gamed by any individual prime.

2. Real-time visibility into how one party’s decisions are creating cost or delay for others.

3. An enforcement layer that ties a meaningful portion of each party’s outcome to the integrity of the whole sequence — not just their isolated scope.

When these three conditions exist, independent contractors begin to behave as if the project were a single system — not because they suddenly became a team, but because the economics and information now reward them for doing so.

WHO THIS IS FOR

This is for owners, developers, and project leads who are tired of absorbing the cost of structural misalignment while every contractor on the job can still claim they “met their contract.”

It is for people who understand that in multi-prime environments, the difference between a 12% overrun and a 3% overrun is rarely better contractors. It is almost always better architecture between them.

The industry already knows how to build. What it has not yet built is the operating layer that makes fragmented execution converge instead of collapse.

That is the work.
